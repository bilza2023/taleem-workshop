## Deck Script

**Two Families of Numbers – Rational and Irrational**

### Slide 1 — Introduction (0–8)

"Mathematics has many kinds of numbers, but one important way to understand them is to divide them into two families.

The first family is called **rational numbers**.

The second family is called **irrational numbers**.

Both of these together form what we call the **real number system**."

---

### Slide 2 — Rational Numbers (8–18)

"Let’s begin with **rational numbers**.

A rational number is any number that can be written as a **fraction of two integers**.

For example, one half, three fourths, or five.

Even whole numbers are rational because they can always be written as a fraction.

Another way to recognize rational numbers is through their **decimal form**.

Their decimals either **end**, like 0.5, or they **repeat**, like 0.333 repeating."

---

### Slide 3 — Irrational Numbers (18–28)

"But not all numbers behave like this.

Some numbers cannot be written as fractions at all.

Numbers like **square root of two**, **pi**, and **e** belong to a different family.

These are called **irrational numbers**.

Their decimal digits **never end and never repeat**.

They go on forever without forming a repeating pattern."

---

### Slide 4 — Geometry Example (28–36)

"One of the most famous irrational numbers appears in a very simple geometric shape.

Consider a **square with side length 1**."

---

### Slide 5 — Square Root of Two (36–48)

"If we draw a diagonal across this square, we can calculate its length using the **Pythagorean theorem**.

The theorem tells us that:

one squared plus one squared equals two.

So the diagonal of this square is **square root of two**."

---

### Slide 6 — Why √2 is Irrational (48–60)

"This raises an interesting question.

Can the number square root of two be written as a fraction?

Mathematicians studied this question carefully.

They proved that **no fraction squared will ever equal two**.

Because of this, square root of two cannot be written as a ratio of integers.

Therefore, it is an **irrational number**."

---

### Slide 7 — Decimal Behavior (60–70)

"There is another useful way to understand the difference between rational and irrational numbers.

We can look at how their **decimals behave**."

---

### Slide 8 — Rational Decimal Pattern (70–82)

"For rational numbers, the decimals always follow a pattern.

Sometimes the decimal **terminates**, like 0.5.

Sometimes it **repeats**, like 0.333 repeating.

But eventually the digits settle into a predictable pattern."

---

### Slide 9 — Irrational Decimal Pattern (82–94)

"Irrational numbers behave differently.

Take square root of two.

Its decimal form begins as 1.41421356…

The digits **never repeat** and **never stop**.

This endless, non-repeating pattern is the hallmark of irrational numbers."

---

### Slide 10 — Final Understanding (94–104)

"So we can summarize the idea simply.

**Rational numbers** can be written as fractions and their decimals terminate or repeat.

**Irrational numbers** cannot be written as fractions and their decimals never repeat.

Together, rational and irrational numbers form the complete system of **real numbers**."
